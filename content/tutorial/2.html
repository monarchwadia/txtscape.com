Your First Room

Describe a tavern in plain text. Your agent will remember it forever.

Create a page

You're going to describe the starting location of your game. Not in code — in a txtscape page. Open your AI chat and say:

Tell your agent
"Create a txtscape page at rooms/tavern.txt with this content:

The Rusty Flagon is a dimly lit tavern. Candles flicker on rough wooden tables. A grizzled bartender polishes a cracked mug behind the counter. The air smells of ale and smoke.

Exits: north leads to the Dark Forest.

Items: a rusty sword hangs on the wall behind the bar."

The agent calls the put_page tool and creates a file at .txtscape/pages/rooms/tavern.txt. You can open it in your editor — it's just a text file:

.txtscape/pages/rooms/tavern.txt

The Rusty Flagon is a dimly lit tavern. Candles flicker
on rough wooden tables. A grizzled bartender polishes a
cracked mug behind the counter. The air smells of ale
and smoke.

Exits: north leads to the Dark Forest.

Items: a rusty sword hangs on the wall behind the bar.

Read it back

Now verify your agent can access the page:

Tell your agent
"Read the rooms/tavern page from txtscape."

The agent calls get_page and reads the tavern description back to you. Same content, retrieved from your project's persistent memory.

What just happened

You stored game knowledge outside your code. This page lives in your repo as a plain .txt file. It persists across conversations. Tomorrow, next week, a month from now — when any AI agent opens this project, the tavern is still there.

This is the core idea: txtscape is persistent memory for your AI agent. Knowledge goes in as pages. The agent reads them whenever it needs context.

You could have created the file manually in your editor instead of asking the agent. txtscape pages are just files — there's nothing magical about how they're created. The agent uses MCP tools, but you can always edit them directly.
